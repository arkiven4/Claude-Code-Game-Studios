# The Witch and the Mage — Love, Loss, and Erasure

**Canon Level**: Established (core canon)
**Dependencies**: [Church Lore](church-lore.md), [Magical Creatures Lore](magical-creatures-lore.md)
**Used In**: Witch Prologue, Chapter 6, Chapter 7, Chapter 8, Chapter 9, Chapter 10, Chapter 11

---

## Overview

The Witch was once an ordinary woman who loved an extraordinary man. The Mage was a scholar of magic — not a creature of it, but a student who believed magical energy could be understood, harnessed, and used to improve human life. The Church executed him for being too powerful, too independent, and too popular among the common people. They harvested his magical essence to fuel their experiments. The Witch survived, broke, and spent a decade building a ritual that would eliminate all magical creatures from the world — not out of hatred, but out of love. If the world was going to turn magic into a tool for control, she would remove magic from the equation entirely. No more magical creatures to suffer. No more essence to harvest. No more Mages to execute. Her goal and the game's ending are the same event: when the Witch dies, all magical creatures vanish. Including Evelyn.

## Detailed Lore

### Who the Witch Was

Before she was the Witch, she was a **healer**. Not a magical one — a practical one. She lived in a village on the edge of the Deep Weald, treating wounds, setting bones, mixing remedies from forest herbs. She was known for her steady hands and her willingness to treat anyone, regardless of their ability to pay.

She was not born with magical ability. She learned magic from the Mage, who was himself self-taught. Her magical education was informal — late-night conversations, shared books, experiments conducted in the garden behind their cabin. She was a slow learner but a deep one. Where the Mage saw magic as a system to be understood, the Witch saw it as a language to be felt. She never matched his raw power, but she developed something he lacked: **intuition**. She could sense magical energy the way a musician senses a wrong note — not by calculation, but by feel.

### Who the Mage Was

The Mage was a **scholar-warrior** — a rare combination of intellectual brilliance and practical magical ability. He believed that magical energy was a natural resource, like water or wind, and that humanity's relationship with it should be one of **partnership** rather than fear or exploitation.

He was charismatic without being ambitious. He traveled between villages, teaching farmers how to use minor magical enhancements to improve crop yields, helping builders reinforce structures with woven enchantments, healing the sick with techniques that blended medical knowledge and magical energy. People loved him not because he demanded it, but because he made their lives tangibly better.

He and the Witch met when he came to her village to teach. She was skeptical — she had seen what happened when untrained people tried to use magic — but she was also curious. Their first conversation lasted six hours. Their first argument lasted three days. They were inseparable after that.

### Their Relationship

The Mage and the Witch were not married in any formal sense. They did not need to be. Their bond was the kind that made formalities irrelevant. They lived together in a cabin on the edge of the Weald — close enough to civilization to help people, close enough to the wild to study magic in its natural state.

Their relationship was built on **shared wonder**. They explored magical theory together, debated ethics late into the night, and disagreed passionately about methodology. The Mage believed in teaching magic to anyone who asked. The Witch believed in screening students for emotional stability first. They never resolved this disagreement, and they both knew it would matter someday.

The Mage kept a staff — not a weapon, but a **focus tool**, carved from a Weald tree and inscribed with runes of his own design. It amplified and directed his magical energy with precision. The staff became a symbol of his philosophy: magic should be guided, not forced.

### The Execution

The Church came for the Mage because he was becoming a **threat** — not a military one, but an ideological one. The Church's doctrine taught that magic was dangerous, that magical creatures were abominations, and that the Church was humanity's sole protector. The Mage was proving, through his actions, that magic could be safe, that it could help people, and that the Church was not necessary.

The Church did not accuse him publicly. They came at night, with a squad of knights and a contingent of ritualists. The Witch was away — gathering herbs in the Weald, a trip that saved her life. She returned to find the cabin ransacked, the Mage gone, and his broken staff left on the floor as a message.

She tracked them to a Church outpost — a small facility where they were holding the Mage before transferring him to a Major Facility for experimentation. She arrived as the **Knight Captain** was preparing to move the prisoner. What followed was not a rescue. It was a massacre.

The Witch unleashed magical power she did not know she possessed. Grief, rage, and love fused into a force that tore through the outpost's defenses. The Knight Captain died fighting her — he believed he was protecting the world from a dangerous sorceress, and he died with that conviction intact. The Witch killed him without a second thought. He was in her way.

She found the Mage's cell too late. The Church had already begun the extraction process. They had not yet completed it, but the ritual had damaged him beyond recovery. The last thing he said to her was not a plea or a warning. It was an apology: *"I'm sorry I couldn't show you everything."*

The Church completed the extraction posthumously. They harvested the Mage's essence — the most concentrated magical energy they had ever obtained — and stored it in their central vault. The Witch watched from a distance, powerless to stop it, and made her vow.

### The Vow

Standing in the burning ruins of the Church outpost, the Witch made a promise — not to the Mage, not to herself, but to the world:

> *"I will burn the parchment."*

The metaphor was deliberate. The Church wrote its control on parchment — treaties, doctrines, experimental records. The world itself was the parchment on which magic was written. If she burned it — if she erased magic from the world entirely — no one would ever suffer the way the Mage suffered again. No magical creature would be created, harvested, or controlled. No one would lose what she lost.

It was not a rational decision. It was not meant to be. It was the decision of someone who had lost the only person who made the world make sense.

### Survival and Breaking Point

The Witch spent the next **ten years** preparing. She did not act immediately because the ritual she envisioned was beyond anything ever attempted. It required:

1. **Understanding the magical substrate:** She mapped the world's magical ley lines, identified the points where magical energy entered the physical world, and developed a theoretical model for how the connection could be severed.
2. **Accumulating power:** The Mage's death had shown her that grief could be converted into magical energy. She spent a decade channeling her grief — not suppressing it, not moving on from it, but *using* it. Every memory of the Mage, every night spent alone in their cabin, every morning she woke and remembered he was gone — all of it became fuel.
3. **Building her stronghold:** She established a base of operations in a region of concentrated magical energy — a place where the boundary between the physical world and the magical substrate was thinnest. Her stronghold is part fortress, part ritual site, part memorial. The Mage's broken staff sits at its center, preserved in a field of suspended time.
4. **Gathering forces:** The Witch did not work alone. She attracted followers — people who had also lost loved ones to the Church's experimentation, magical creatures who feared being captured, disillusioned Church defectors who had learned the truth. Her forces are not an army in the conventional sense. They are a **movement** — united not by loyalty to her, but by shared grief and shared purpose.

Her breaking point was not a single moment. It was the accumulation of a decade of watching the Church grow more powerful, more brazen, and more corrupt. Every new monster the Church created, every village it manipulated, every life it destroyed was another brick in the wall of her resolve.

### Her Resolution

The Witch's goal is absolute: **eliminate all magical creatures from the world**. Not kill them — *eliminate* them. Remove the magical energy that animates them, so that they simply cease to have ever existed in their current form.

She does not hate magical creatures. She **pities** them. She sees them as victims of a world that created them only to exploit them. The Church makes them for control. Villagers fear them for existing. They are born into suffering and die into nothing. The Witch's plan is, in her mind, an act of mercy. She is ending their suffering by ending their existence.

The fact that this also eliminates Evelyn — a magical creature who has found love, purpose, and connection — is a tragedy the Witch acknowledges but accepts. In her calculus, no individual happiness justifies the continuation of a system that produces industrial-scale suffering. She is willing to be the villain of Evelyn's story if it means there will never be another Evelyn.

### Her Philosophy: "Right, Just Wrong About the Method"

The Witch's diagnosis is correct:

- The Church *is* corrupt. It *does* create monsters to control people.
- Magical creatures *are* suffering. They *are* treated as resources.
- The world *is* broken in its relationship with magic.

Her cure is wrong:

- Eliminating magic eliminates the good along with the bad. The Mage's dream of using magic to help people dies with it.
- Evelyn dies. Party members who are magical creatures die. Innocent beings who have done nothing wrong cease to exist.
- The world that remains is safe but diminished. It is a world without monsters, but also without wonder.

The Witch knows this. She has always known this. She chose this path not because it was right, but because it was the only one she could see through her grief. She was right about the problem. She was wrong about the solution. And she is powerful enough to enact it anyway.

### Power Level and Capabilities

The Witch is the most powerful magical entity in the game, surpassing even Primal Beasts in raw output (though not in ontological permanence — she is mortal, and she can be killed). Her abilities include:

- **Reality Warping:** She can reshape the physical environment on a local scale — raising walls, collapsing floors, redirecting waterways. Her stronghold is a testament to this power.
- **Energy Projection:** She can fire concentrated blasts of magical energy, create shields, and generate areas of effect that disrupt enemy abilities.
- **Emotional Resonance:** Her magic is tied to her emotional state. The more she feels, the more powerful she becomes. This is both her greatest strength and her greatest vulnerability — moments of emotional clarity (conversations with Evelyn, seeing Evan's devotion) can destabilize her power.
- **Ritual Anchoring:** She is the living anchor for the Vanishing ritual. As long as she lives, the ritual is incomplete. When she dies, it completes automatically.
- **Shadow Forces:** Her followers and corrupted magical creatures act as extensions of her will across the world. The "Witch's Shadow" that spreads in Chapters 6-8 is not metaphorical — it is her magical influence seeping into areas, warping them, drawing in those who share her grief.

## Mechanical Implications

- **The Witch is not fought until Chapter 11.** Earlier encounters (Chapter 8) are narrative-first — she retreats because she does not want to fight the party, not because she cannot defeat them.
- **The Witch's power level** means the final boss fight must be designed around her *choosing* to hold back, not being evenly matched. The party wins because she lets them, not because they overpower her.
- **Her emotional vulnerability** is the key to the mercy-killing ending. The final dialogue must reach her emotionally before the combat ends — she thanks them because they showed her that the Mage's dream (magic helping people) was not entirely wrong.
- **The Vanishing trigger** is her death. This means the boss fight's victory condition is also the narrative's point of no return. There is no "spare the Witch" option — sparing her means the Vanishing never completes, but it also means she continues her campaign. The game does not offer this branch.
- **Her stronghold** (Chapter 10-11) should visually reflect her nature: part memorial, part fortress, part ritual site. The Mage's preserved staff at its center is a key visual anchor.
