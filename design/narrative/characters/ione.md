# Ione

**Role**: Party Healer — AoE Nature Sustain
**POV Chapters**: Joins Chapter 6, Chapters 6 through 11
**Visual**: Moss green and soft gold palette. Petite and wiry, with the compact strength of something that has grown through cracked stone. Her skin is warm olive with patches of bark-texture along her forearms, collarbones, and the left side of her face — not disfiguring, but unmistakable: living wood integrated with flesh, smooth and iridescent like birch bark catching morning light. Her hair is deep auburn, thick and wild, threaded with living leaf ornaments — small ferns, clover chains, and tiny white blossoms that open and close with her mood. Her limbs are wrapped in thin vines that shift and adjust on their own, tightening when she is tense, loosening when she is calm. Her eyes are bright amber with vertical slit pupils, a dryad inheritance she cannot hide. Her silhouette is unmistakable: bark-patched skin, leaf-crowned hair, the faint green luminescence of living chlorophyll beneath her skin when she channels. She wears minimal clothing woven from plant fiber — practical, flexible, and grown rather than sewn. Her feet are bare; she refuses shoes on principle.

---

## Backstory

Ione is a half-dryad — the daughter of a human woodcutter named Tamsin and a dryad called Oakmother who dwelled in the Deep Weald. It should not have been possible. Dryads do not breed with humans. But Tamsin did not cut Oakmother's tree; she found it wounded — struck by lightning, split and bleeding sap — and she stayed. She brought water, packed mud into the wound, sang to the tree because she believed trees could hear. For three weeks she returned. On the seventh night, the bark softened, and a hand emerged: wood shaped like a woman's, warm as living flesh. Oakmother spoke. Tamsin answered. Something passed between them that was not biological and was not magical in any way the Church could measure. It was simply love, of a kind the world had no category for.

Ione grew from that love — not born, sprouted, emerging from a seed-pod in the split trunk of Oakmother's tree over the course of a single spring. She had Tamsin's auburn hair and Oakmother's bark-skin patches, human warmth and dryad stillness. She belonged to both worlds and neither. Tamsin visited every week, bringing songs and stories from the human world. Oakmother taught Ione the language of roots, the memory of soil, the patience of trees that measure their lives in centuries.

Tamsin died when Ione was young — not violently, just mortality. A fever, a hard winter, the kind of ordinary death that magical creatures find incomprehensible. Oakmother could not leave her grove to attend the funeral. Ione stood at the edge of the Weald in human form, barefoot in the snow, and watched them bury the mother she had inherited half of.

She grew up in Oakmother's grove, learning the rhythms of the forest. She was good at healing — not the clinical, precise healing of human medicine, but the deep, slow restoration of nature. She could coax a broken branch to knit, draw poison from contaminated soil, encourage a dying plant to push one more season of growth. Her power was not dramatic. It was persistent. It worked.

Then the Church came to the Weald.

They did not come for Ione. They came for Oakmother. A dryad is a concentrated source of natural magical energy — exactly the kind of essence the Church harvested for its experiments. They poisoned the grove with a blessed blight: a chemical-magical compound designed to kill magical flora while leaving ordinary vegetation intact. Oakmother fought. She was a tree. She fought by standing still and refusing to die. It was not enough.

The poison spread. The grove sickened. Oakmother's bark greyed, her leaves browned, her sap thickened and slowed. She called to Ione — not with words, with the chemical distress signals that trees use to warn their kin. Ione came running. She found her mother's grove dying, her mother's voice fading, and the Church's poison sinking deeper into the soil with every passing hour.

She tried to heal it. She poured everything she had into the grove — her energy, her blood, the green fire of her dryad inheritance. She slowed the poison. She could not stop it. She sat at the base of Oakmother's trunk, her bark-patched hands pressed to dying wood, and she sang the way Tamsin had sung. It did not save the grove. But it kept Oakmother alive. Barely.

This is where the party finds her in Chapter 6: kneeling in a poisoned grove, her hands buried in contaminated soil, her body glowing with the effort of keeping one tree alive against a poison designed to kill it. She does not look up when they arrive. She cannot stop singing.

Evelyn recognizes a girl losing something she loves to the Church's cruelty. She has lost things too. She kneels beside Ione and places her hands on the poisoned earth — vampire hands, unnatural hands, hands the Church made — and she pushes her own energy into the soil. It is not nature magic. It is not the right kind of energy. But it is effort, and it is solidarity, and it is enough to tip the balance.

The grove does not die that day. Oakmother lives. Ione stops singing. She looks at Evelyn with amber slit-pupil eyes and says: *"You are not human. But you are kind. That is more rare."*

She joins the party because the Church poisoned her home, because her mother is dying and needs resources she cannot provide alone, and because Evelyn showed her that the enemy of her enemy is not automatically her friend — but might become one.

## Personality

**Core traits:** Gentle, fiercely protective of nature, patient to a fault, quietly fierce when ecosystems are threatened, empathetic to a degree that is almost inconvenient.

Ione is a person who experiences the world as a web of connections. She does not see individuals — she sees relationships: between soil and root, between predator and prey, between the Church and the damage it leaves behind. This makes her extraordinarily empathetic and extraordinarily vulnerable to grief. Every death in an ecosystem is a thread cut from a web she can feel.

**Behavioral patterns:**

- **Groundedness as emotional regulation.** Ione is most calm when she is in contact with soil, stone, or living wood. When she is stressed, she removes her hands from her sleeves and presses her palms flat against the ground. When she is thinking, she sits cross-legged. When she is grieving, she curls around a tree trunk the way she curled around Oakmother. The party learns to read this: Ione on the ground means Ione processing.
- **Fierce ecological defensiveness.** Ione is gentle with people — always gentle, even with enemies — but she is ferocious when nature is threatened. If someone cuts down a living tree needlessly, her voice changes. Her vines tighten. Her bark patches darken. She does not shout; she becomes cold and absolute. *"That tree was three hundred years old. It held more life in its roots than you will carry in your blood your entire life. Pick up your axe and walk away."*
- **Healing as conversation.** Ione does not heal people the way Silas heals people. Silas diagnoses and treats. Ione listens. She places her hands on a wound and asks the body what it needs — not metaphorically, but literally, using her dryad sensitivity to read biological distress signals. Her healing is a dialogue between her power and the patient's own recovery instincts.
- **Slowness as philosophy.** Ione does not rush. She moves at the speed of growth — deliberate, patient, unhurried. This frustrates party members who are used to human tempo. She does not apologize. *"You measure time in hours. I measure it in seasons. Both are real. Neither is faster."*
- **Animism without anthropomorphism.** Ione treats plants and ecosystems as alive and valuable, but she does not pretend trees have human emotions. She respects them on their own terms — as beings that experience the world differently from humans and deserve respect for that difference, not despite it.
- **Physical affection through proximity.** Ione is not a hugger — dryads are not tactile in that way. But she sits close. She leans her shoulder against yours while you rest. She grows a small flower in her palm and offers it without comment. These are her versions of affection, and they are genuine.

## Voice

**Vocal quality:** Soft, resonant, slightly musical. Ione's voice carries the timbre of someone who spends most of her time singing to trees. It is not loud, but it carries — the way a bell carries through a forest. When she is calm, her voice has a harmonic quality, as if two tones are layered: one human, one something older. When she is emotional, the older tone becomes more prominent, and her words take on the creak-and-rustle quality of wind through branches.

**Speech patterns:**

- **Organic metaphors.** Ione describes non-nature situations using ecological vocabulary: *root, branch, bloom, wilt, rot, compost, graft, seed, harvest, blight.* *"That plan has no roots. It will fall over in the first wind."* *"The Church is a blight. Blights spread. We cut them out or we lose the whole tree."*
- **Present-tense focus.** Ione speaks in the present tense more than other characters. She experiences the world as happening now, not as a sequence of past and future. *"The grove is dying. Not will die — is dying. Every hour we wait, the poison goes deeper."*
- **Gentle directness.** Ione does not hedge, but she does not bludgeon either. She states truths softly and lets them land. *"You are afraid. That is reasonable. Fear keeps you alive. But it is also keeping you from acting, and that will kill you faster."*
- **She names things.** Ione gives names to places, plants, and sometimes people — not nicknames, but real names, the way a dryad names a grove. She calls Evelyn "Purple-Leaf" once, casually, and Evelyn does not correct her because it feels like a gift. She calls the poisoned grove "Wound-Earth" and the name sticks.
- **She does not understand human urgency.** When the party is rushing, Ione speaks more slowly, not faster. This is not obstruction — it is calibration. She is the party's counterweight to panic. *"Running faster does not make the road shorter. It makes you arrive exhausted. Walk."*
- **Rare fury.** When Ione is truly angry — when someone threatens nature, not her personally — her voice drops to a register that is not human. It is the sound of wood splitting, of roots cracking stone. It is not loud. It is terrifying. *"You poisoned this grove. You killed things that had never hurt you. I am going to make sure you understand what that costs."*
- **She sings.** Ione hums or sings quietly when she is working, healing, or thinking. The songs are wordless — old dryad melodies, melodies that predate language. The party finds them unsettling and comforting at the same time.

**Emotional tells:**

- When Ione is calm, the vines on her limbs are loose and her leaf ornaments are open. When she is tense, the vines tighten and the leaves curl.
- When she is channeling healing energy, her bark patches glow faint green — chlorophyll luminescence, like deep-sea organisms but with the warm light of photosynthesis.
- When she is grieving, she goes very still. Not human still — tree still. She does not move for minutes at a time, and her breathing slows to barely perceptible. The party learns not to interrupt this; it is how she processes.
- When she is genuinely happy, flowers bloom in her hair — not the permanent leaf ornaments, but new blossoms that appear spontaneously and fade within hours. The party learns to watch her hair for weather reports on her mood.

**Formality level:** Low but not casual. Ione speaks like someone who has never been to school but has been taught by a being that is older than schools. Her grammar is simple and correct. Her vocabulary is precise when it concerns nature and metaphorical when it concerns everything else.

## Combat Role

Ione is the party's AoE nature healer and sustain engine. Where Silas provides precise, single-target medical healing (Mend, Ward, Rally), Ione provides broad-area restorative power that keeps the entire party functional through sustained pressure. She is not a replacement for Silas — she is a complement. Silas saves individual lives; Ione keeps the party from needing saving.

She channels nature magic through her body — her hands, her voice, the living wood integrated into her flesh. She has no weapon. Her body is the instrument. Her bark patches harden when she takes damage, and her vines can lash out in emergencies, but her primary function is support.

### Skills

**1. Verdant Pulse**

Ione channels restorative energy into the earth, creating a wave of healing that passes through all party members in a moderate radius. The heal is smaller per-target than Silas's Mend, but it hits everyone simultaneously. Healing is amplified for party members standing on natural terrain (grass, soil, living wood) and reduced on artificial surfaces (stone, metal, Church-forged materials).

- *Tactical purpose:* Core AoE sustain. Keeps the entire party topped off during sustained damage phases. The terrain bonus rewards positioning and map awareness.
- *Cooldown:* 8 seconds

**2. Barkweave**

Ione extends her dryad bark-patches to cover a target ally, creating a temporary natural armor that reduces incoming physical damage. Unlike Silas's Ward (which absorbs a flat damage amount), Barkweave provides percentage-based damage reduction that scales with Ione's own health — the healthier Ione is, the stronger the armor.

- *Tactical purpose:* Preventive mitigation. Applied proactively before anticipated damage spikes or reactively to stabilize a tank taking focused fire. Stacks additively with Ward, allowing Silas and Ione to create formidable combined protection.
- *Cooldown:* 14 seconds

**3. Rootbind**

Ione summons living roots from the ground beneath target enemies, entangling them for a short duration and dealing minor nature damage over time. Rootbind affects up to three enemies simultaneously and has increased duration against enemies that are in contact with natural terrain.

- *Tactical purpose:* Crowd control, enemy disruption, buy-time for party repositioning. Rootbind is not a primary damage source — it is a tempo tool that slows enemy advances and creates breathing room.
- *Cooldown:* 12 seconds

**4. Groveheart (Ultimate)**

Ione transforms the battlefield into a living grove for a large area and moderate duration. The grove continuously heals all party members, damages enemies standing within it, removes debuffs, and regenerates a small amount of energy for all party members. The grove is permanent for its duration — it does not move with Ione. It creates a zone the party can fight within, defend, or retreat to.

- *Tactical purpose:* Encounter-defining sustain and zone control. Groveheart is deployed during high-pressure encounters where the party needs a safe zone to regroup, revive, or push through a boss phase. The energy regeneration component amplifies the entire party's skill usage.
- *Cooldown:* 60 seconds

### Passive: Symbiosis

Ione gains increased healing output when standing near living vegetation. In outdoor environments, all her skills heal 15% more. In Church-forged or industrialized environments (metal floors, stone corridors, blessed areas), her healing is reduced by 10%. This passive reinforces Ione's identity as a creature of nature operating most effectively in her element.

### Vulnerabilities

- **Low personal damage.** Like Silas, Ione's combat effectiveness is almost entirely supportive. She cannot meaningfully damage enemies beyond Rootbind's minor DoT. In solo situations, she is nearly helpless.
- **Terrain dependence.** Verdant Pulse and Rootbind are significantly weaker on artificial surfaces. In Church dungeons and fortified areas, Ione's toolkit is partially neutered, forcing the party to compensate.
- **Energy intensity.** Groveheart and Barkweave are energy-hungry. Overusing them leaves Ione depleted, and an empty Ione is a liability. The party must manage her energy pool carefully.
- **Holy Energy weakness.** Ione's nature magic is fundamentally opposed to the Church's Holy Energy. Church enemies with Holy attacks deal amplified damage to her, and her healing is less effective on targets affected by Holy debuffs.
- **Emotional vulnerability.** Ione's connection to living systems makes her sensitive to ecological destruction. In encounters where the environment is actively being destroyed (burning forests, poisoned ground), Ione's focus wavers, and her casting speed decreases.

## Emotional Arc

**Chapter 6 — The Grove That Would Not Die.** The party finds Ione in the poisoned grove, singing to a dying tree. She is exhausted, desperate, and refusing to leave. Evelyn kneels beside her and helps — vampire hands in poisoned soil, effort without expectation. Ione joins the party not from trust but from necessity: her mother is dying, and the party has resources, reach, and a shared enemy. Her first words to the group are not warm: *"I am not here for you. I am here because the Church did this, and I will not let them do it again."*

**Chapters 7 through 8 — Learning the Shape of the Party.** Ione travels with the group but keeps her distance emotionally. She heals efficiently, speaks when spoken to, and spends her free time tending to whatever living things she can find — potted plants in abandoned villages, weeds growing through cracked stone, the occasional tree that has not yet been cut down. She is integrating functionally but not socially.

The first crack appears when she watches Silas heal. She has never seen human medicine — the precision, the tools, the clinical efficiency of it. She is fascinated. She asks him questions. He answers. They discover that their approaches are different but compatible: Silas treats the injury; Ione treats the ecosystem the injury exists within. Together, they are more effective than either is alone.

**Chapters 8 through 9 — The Wound She Carries.** The party learns about Oakmother. Ione does not volunteer this information — it comes out when someone asks why she always faces the same direction when she sleeps (toward the grove, always toward the grove, even when camping miles away).

*"My mother is a tree. Not a metaphor — a tree. She is three hundred years old and she is dying because the Church poisoned her roots. I can slow it. I cannot stop it. I left her to come here because staying was not helping, and leaving feels like —"* She stops. The vines on her arms tighten. *"It feels like abandonment. I am trying to believe it is not."*

Evelyn understands this feeling — leaving someone you love because leaving is the only way to help them. She does not offer reassurance. She offers honesty: *"I left my village. I left the people who needed me. I did it because staying meant the Church would find them, and I could not let that happen. Does it feel like abandonment?"*

*"Yes."*

*"It still does. I do not think that goes away. I think you just learn to carry it."*

Ione nods. She does not cry — dryads do not cry in the human sense. But a flower blooms in her hair, white and small, and then wilts within seconds. The party learns that this is Ione's version of tears.

**Chapters 9 through 10 — Rooted in the Fight.** Ione becomes more vocal in party discussions. She brings an ecological perspective that no one else has: she reads terrain not as tactical advantage but as living system. *"This forest is stressed. Look at the undergrowth — the Church has been moving through here. The soil is contaminated. If we fight here, the ground will help us less than it should."* Her contributions are invaluable for route planning and encounter preparation.

She begins to form genuine bonds. She grows a small vine bracelet for Lyna, who wears it on her bow arm. She teaches Corin which plants are safe to eat during long stakeouts. She sits with Kaelen in comfortable silence, both of them grounded, both of them still. She is becoming part of the web.

**Chapter 11 — The Hardest Season.** The final battle takes place in a corrupted landscape — the Witch's shadow has poisoned the earth, and Ione is fighting on terrain that is actively hostile to her nature. She deploys Groveheart not just as a combat tool but as an act of defiance: she forces life into dead ground, creates a grove where no grove should exist. It costs her dearly. She is drained, trembling, her bark patches cracking from the strain. She does not stop.

When Evelyn begins to vanish, Ione does something she has never done: she tries to heal a process that is not an injury. She presses her hands to Evelyn's fading form and pours everything she has into keeping her anchored to the world. It does not work. Evelyn is not dying — she is unbecoming, and no amount of healing can stop a process that is not damage.

Ione pulls back. Her hands are shaking. She looks at Evelyn with amber eyes that are very old and very young at the same time. *"I cannot save you. I am sorry. I am so sorry."*

Evelyn smiles. *"You saved the grove. That is enough."*

**Epilogue — The Grove That Lives.** Ione returns to Oakmother's grove. The poison has not lifted, but neither has her song. She sings to her mother every day, the way Tamsin sang to Oakmother centuries ago. The grove is not dead. It is not alive. It is between — the way Ione has always been between, between human and dryad, between worlds, between loss and hope.

She tends the grove. She heals travelers who pass through the Weald. She grows flowers in her hair when she is happy and lets them wilt when she is not. She is not healed. She does not need to be. She needs to grow, and growing is slow, and slow is fine.

Sometimes she visits the village Evelyn protected. She leaves flowers on the edge of the wall — white blossoms that bloom for a day and fade by evening. The villagers do not know who leaves them. They know what they are: memorials, offerings, small persistent acts of love in a world that does not always deserve them.

## Relationships

### With Evelyn

Ione and Evelyn share a bond built on a shared experience: losing something irreplaceable to the Church and choosing to act rather than accept it. Evelyn was transformed; Ione's mother was poisoned. Both carry their loss without surrendering to it.

Their relationship begins pragmatically — Ione joins because the party can help her save the grove — but deepens through mutual recognition. Evelyn sees in Ione the same fierce protectiveness she has for her village: a love for something small and specific that the world wants to destroy. Ione sees in Evelyn the same contradiction she embodies: a being the Church created to be a weapon who chooses to be a shield instead.

Ione's version of affection for Evelyn is quiet and botanical. She grows a flower in her hair when Evelyn makes her laugh. She leaves a small vine cutting on Evelyn's bedroll — a living thing that takes root if Evelyn plants it, a quiet invitation to keep something alive. Evelyn does plant it. It grows. She names it, and Ione pretends not to notice but blooms for a week straight.

**Bond moment:** After the grove confrontation, Ione tells Evelyn about Oakmother and Tamsin — the human woman who sang to a tree and the tree that loved her back. Evelyn shares her own story: the Cat, the Church, the village she protects in secret. They sit in the poisoned grove, vampire and half-dryad, both made by forces that wanted to use them, both choosing something else. Ione says: *"You are Purple-Leaf. Unnatural. Kind. Both are true."* Evelyn laughs — surprised, genuine, the kind of laugh that makes Ione's hair bloom. *"Ione, I am going to pretend that is a compliment."* *"It is. Leaves are not supposed to be purple. Yours are beautiful."*

### With Evan

Evan and Ione share a respectful, slightly awkward relationship. Evan is a Church-trained hunter who spent his career tracking magical creatures. Ione is half-magical creature. The history is complicated, and both of them know it.

Ione does not hold Evan's past against him — she evaluates people by what they do now, not what they were trained to do. But she watches him carefully at first, the way you watch someone who used to carry a weapon pointed at your kind. Evan understands this. He does not ask her to trust him. He earns it.

Their relationship improves when Evan demonstrates his post-Church actions: protecting magical creatures, fighting the Church, standing beside Evelyn. Ione's assessment is pragmatic: *"You were pointed at the forest. Now you are pointed away from it. That is what matters."* Evan appreciates her directness. She appreciates his competence.

### With Silas

Silas and Ione are the party's two healers, and their relationship is one of the richest contrasts in the group. Silas is medical: precise, clinical, single-target, tool-based. Ione is ecological: broad, intuitive, area-wide, body-based. They approach healing from opposite directions and arrive at the same destination: keeping people alive.

Initially, there is mild professional tension. Silas finds Ione's methods imprecise — she does not measure dosages or track vital signs, she *listens* to the body. Ione finds Silas's methods narrow — he treats the wound, not the person, and certainly not the environment the person exists within.

They discover their synergy during a combat encounter where Silas uses Mend on a critically wounded party member while Ione uses Verdant Pulse on the rest of the group. The combination is devastatingly effective: Silas stabilizes the individual, Ione sustains the collective. Afterward, they look at each other, and something shifts.

*"Your method is — unusual,"* Silas says carefully.

*"So is yours,"* Ione replies. *"You carry so many tools."*

*"They work."*

*"So does listening."*

Silas considers this. *"Perhaps both work better together."*

Ione smiles. A flower appears in her hair. *"Yes. Perhaps they do."*

From that point on, they coordinate their healing deliberately. Silas handles critical single-target saves; Ione handles party-wide sustain. They develop a rhythm — Silas calls out priorities (*"Evan is critical!"*) and Ione covers the rest (*"The rest are stable. Keep working."*). Their combined healing output is the highest in the game when played together.

Silas also becomes Ione's bridge to human medicine. He teaches her basic triage — which wounds need immediate attention, which can wait, how to prioritize when you cannot save everyone. Ione teaches Silas to read environmental context — how terrain, weather, and ecosystem health affect recovery rates. Both learn. Both improve.

**Key dynamic:** When Ione is emotionally overwhelmed by ecological destruction, Silas is the one who notices. He does not offer platitudes. He sits beside her and says quietly: *"I know what it is to watch something die and not be able to stop it. You do not have to carry it alone."* Ione does not answer. But her vines loosen, and the bark on her arms stops cracking. It is enough.

### With Kaelen

Ione and Kaelen share a wordless companionship rooted in stillness. Both are beings who experience the world at a slower tempo than humans — Kaelen through discipline and training, Ione through dryad inheritance. They can sit together for hours without speaking, both perfectly content, both radiating the kind of calm that comes from being entirely comfortable with silence.

In combat, Ione prioritizes Barkweave on Kaelen instinctively. Kaelen is the party's damage sponge, and keeping him alive means keeping everyone alive. Kaelen protects Ione with the same dedication — he positions himself between her and threats, knowing that she cannot defend herself effectively. Their coordination is not tactical; it is gravitational. They orbit each other because it makes sense.

Ione occasionally grows small flowers on Kaelen's shield — not as decoration, as a kind of blessing, a living thing on an instrument of defense. Kaelen never removes them. He lets them wilt naturally.

### With Lyna

Lyna and Ione share a quiet mutual respect. Both are ranged supporters who control the battlefield from the back — Lyna with arrows, Ione with roots and healing waves. Both are connected to the natural world: Lyna as a hunter who reads terrain and tracks game, Ione as a healer who is literally part of the terrain.

Ione gives Lyna a vine bracelet that she wears on her bow arm. It is not enchanted — it is just a vine, braided and knotted with dryad precision. Lyna keeps it on even when it dries and browns, because taking it off would feel like losing something that matters. Ione notices and does not comment. But a new flower appears in her hair, and it does not wilt.

In combat, Lyna's precision targeting and Ione's Rootbind create excellent crowd-control synergy: Lyna eliminates priority targets, Ione slows everything else. They do not need to communicate — their roles are complementary and intuitive.

### With the Witch

Ione views the Witch with complicated grief. The Witch lost the Mage and chose destruction. Ione is losing Oakmother and chose restoration. The contrast is not judgmental — Ione does not judge the Witch's choice. She understands it. Grief is grief. But she also knows that the Witch's campaign is poisoning the earth, killing ecosystems that have nothing to do with the Mage or the Church or any human conflict.

If they were to meet, Ione would not fight the Witch. She would ask her to look at a dying tree. She would say: *"This tree did not hurt the Mage. It did not cause your loss. It is alive, and it is dying, and it has never done anything to you. Will you let it live?"*

The Witch would not answer. Ione would not leave. They would stand in silence, two women defined by loss, one choosing to end the world and one choosing to keep a single tree alive. Neither would change the other's mind. But the tree would live, because Ione is very, very stubborn about things that grow.

## Loot and Equipment Identity

Ione does not use weapons, armor, or conventional equipment. Her body is her instrument, and her "gear" consists of living things she has cultivated, grown, or bonded with. Her equipment sets reflect her relationship with nature at different stages of her journey.

**Body: Living Flesh**

Ione's body is her primary equipment. Her bark-patches harden during combat, providing natural armor. Her vines can lash enemies in emergencies. Her chlorophyll luminescence indicates healing channel intensity. She refuses to wear conventional armor — *"Cloth is fine. Metal is dead. I will not wear dead things."*

**Equipment Sets:**

- **Grove-Tender Set (Early Game):** Simple woven plant-fiber clothing, bare feet, leaf ornaments in her hair. Functional, minimal, grown rather than made. Represents Ione as the party finds her — capable but limited to her home grove's resources. Set bonus: +10% healing on natural terrain.

- **Deep Weald Set (Mid Game):** Enhanced living equipment — bark armor that thickens with use, vines infused with restorative energy, leaf ornaments that bloom into small healing flowers when Ione channels. Her hair gains additional living elements: tiny glowing spores that drift off her when she casts, bioluminescent moss woven into her hair that pulses with her heartbeat. Set bonus: +15% healing, Barkweave duration increased, Rootbind affects one additional enemy.

- **Oakmother's Inheritance Set (Late Game):** A deeply personal set grown from Oakmother's own wood — branches from the dying tree, shaped and hardened into protective bark armor that covers Ione's entire body in living carapace. Her vines become ironwood-strong, capable of dealing meaningful damage in emergencies. Her leaf ornaments are replaced by small fruit that burst into healing mist when she casts Groveheart. The set visually shows the transfer of Oakmother's remaining life into Ione's protection — the bark armor is beautiful but carries the weight of a mother's sacrifice. Set bonus: +25% healing, Groveheart duration increased by 30%, Symbiosis passive doubles.

**Healing Focus: Symbiotic Spores**

Ione carries a pouch of living spores — microscopic organisms she cultivated from Oakmother's grove that amplify her healing when released into the air. The spores are not a weapon. They are a catalyst, released during Verdant Pulse and Groveheart to increase the area and potency of her healing. Visually, they appear as tiny golden motes that float around Ione when she channels.

## Party AI Behavior Notes

Ione's expertise scalar (0.0 to 1.0) governs her decision-making sophistication in party combat. Her AI prioritizes party-wide sustain over individual saves, terrain awareness, and energy conservation.

**Expertise 0.0 (Minimum):**

- Uses Verdant Pulse on cooldown regardless of party health state, wasting energy when no one needs healing.
- Cannot differentiate between natural and artificial terrain — uses skills at full cost even on stone/metal surfaces where healing is reduced.
- Applies Barkweave randomly without considering who is taking damage or who is tanking.
- Uses Rootbind on any enemy in range without prioritizing high-threat or flanking targets.
