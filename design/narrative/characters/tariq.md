# Tariq

**Role**: Party Mage — The Man Who Taught Himself the World
**POV Chapters**: Joins Chapter 8, full presence Chapters 9 through 11
**Visual**: Warm earth and faded indigo palette. Medium height, soft-featured, and comfortably built — not athletic, not frail, but the solid, unremarkable physique of someone who spends more time reading than running. His face is warm: round eyes the colour of dark amber, a broad nose that has been broken once and set slightly crooked, and a mouth that defaults to a small, absent smile — the expression of someone who is always thinking about something just adjacent to the present moment. His skin is a warm brown, the tone of someone who grew up in a sunlit climate, and it is marked with ink stains that never quite wash out: blue on his left thumb, black on his right index finger, a faint purple smudge on his chin that he has not noticed. His hair is dark, curly, and entirely unmanaged — it curls in every direction, occasionally held back by a strip of cloth that has slipped loose and now hangs at an angle. His hands are the most distinctive thing about him: long fingers, ink-stained, calloused from writing, with a silver ring on his right middle finger that he spins when he is thinking. He wears robes — not wizard robes, not ceremonial robes, but a patchwork of comfortable, worn garments he has collected over years of wandering. The robes are layered: an inner tunic of faded indigo, a middle layer of earth-brown wool, and an outer cloak of patchwork fabric with visible repairs. Every pocket contains something: a folded page of notes, a vial of reagent, a piece of chalk, a half-eaten sweet. His silhouette is unmistakable: the curling hair, the patchwork cloak, the way he walks while reading, the ink-stained hands always moving — writing, gesting, spinning his ring. Soft cartoon aesthetic — his warmth reads immediately; his scholarly clutter reads as endearing, not messy.

## Backstory

Tariq was born in a city that no longer exists — not destroyed, just absorbed. A mid-sized trading hub on the edge of Church territory, it was swallowed by Church expansion during the institution's aggressive growth phase. The city's libraries were confiscated, its scholars were reassigned, and its traditions of independent learning were classified as "unlicensed intellectual activity." Tariq was eight when it happened. He remembers the scholars being led away. He remembers the books being loaded onto carts. He remembers his mother — a schoolteacher — telling him to keep reading, no matter what anyone said.

*"They can take the books. They cannot take the reading."*

He kept reading.

Tariq was not born magical. He was not born with essence sensitivity, or magical blood, or any of the markers the Church uses to identify and classify magical talent. He was born curious — a quality the Church found more threatening than any magical ability. While other children played, Tariq read. While other children learned trades, Tariq learned the underlying principles. He read everything he could find: discarded Church manuals (incomplete, redacted, but useful), folk magic texts (imprecise, superstitious, but containing kernels of real technique), and — most valuably — the marginalia. The notes previous readers had written in the margins of books, the corrections, the arguments, the *"this does not work, try this instead."* The marginalia taught him more than the texts.

He taught himself magic. Not the Church's structured, ritualized, approval-required magic — the real thing. The messy, experimental, self-taught magic of people who figured it out by trying and failing and trying again. He learned elemental manipulation from a folk healer's notes on weather patterns. He learned ritual geometry from a disgraced scholar's treatise on sacred architecture. He learned energy channeling from a mage's diary — a woman who had documented her own practice over forty years, writing in code that Tariq spent three years deciphering.

He was not good at first. He was not talented. He was persistent. He burned his hands, singed his eyebrows, flooded his room, set fire to his curtains, and once accidentally turned a pot of stew into a small, confused frog. He kept going.

By his twenties, Tariq was a competent self-taught mage — not powerful in the way Church-trained mages were powerful (structured, efficient, institutionally sanctioned), but effective in the way self-taught people are effective: adaptable, creative, and entirely unbound by what he was "supposed" to do. He could not cast Church spells — he had never been trained in their forms. But he could cast his own: elemental bolts adapted from weather patterns, ritual circles adapted from sacred geometry, shields adapted from architectural load-bearing principles. They were not elegant. They were not efficient. They worked.

He became a hedge wizard — a wandering practitioner who took odd jobs in villages that needed magical help: clearing blight from crops, purifying water, repairing structural damage from storms, the occasional monster deterrence. He did not charge much. He accepted payment in food, lodging, and access to local libraries. He traveled extensively, collecting knowledge the way other people collect coins. Every village had a scholar, a healer, or an old person with interesting marginalia. Tariq found them all.

The Church classified him as an "unlicensed magical practitioner" — not a criminal, not a threat, but a person operating outside Church authority. They monitored him. They did not pursue him aggressively — he was not powerful enough to be a concern, and pursuing him would require resources better spent elsewhere. Tariq knew he was being watched. He did not care. He had work to do.

The Witch's campaign changed the calculus. As magical practitioners were increasingly targeted — both by the Church (as potential Witch allies) and by the Witch's followers (as potential recruits) — Tariq's neutrality became untenable. He was not joining anyone's army. He was not taking sides in a war he did not understand. He was reading, learning, and helping villages that needed him.

He joins Evelyn's party in Chapter 8 when they arrive at a library-sanctuary — a hidden collection of books, scrolls, and magical texts that has been maintained by underground scholars for decades. The sanctuary is under threat: Church forces have identified it and are planning a raid. The party needs the sanctuary's intelligence on Church magical operations. The sanctuary needs someone who can help them defend it and evacuate the most valuable texts. Tariq is that person — he has been a patron of the sanctuary for years, and he knows its collection better than anyone except the head archivist.

*"You need the Church's magical operation files? I know where they are. Third floor, eastern wing, behind the false wall in the geography section. I can get you in. In return: help me move the incunabula. They are eight hundred years old. They are not replaceable. I am."*

He joins because the party gives him access to Church intelligence he cannot reach alone, and because the sanctuary's texts need protecting. He does not call this heroism. He calls it bibliography. It is the closest thing to a cause he has.

## Personality

**Core traits:** Gentle, methodical, intellectually voracious, quietly brave, patient to the point of stubbornness.

Tariq is a man who has spent his life learning and has learned that knowledge is not power — knowledge is responsibility. The more you know, the more you owe. He does not seek power. He seeks understanding. And when understanding reveals a problem, he acts — not heroically, not dramatically, but methodically, the way he does everything.

**Behavioral patterns:**

- **The marginal reader.** Tariq reads everything — but he reads the margins first. The notes, corrections, arguments, and doodles left by previous readers tell him more than the text itself. He applies this to people: he observes their habits, their tells, their off-hand comments, the things they say when they think no one is listening. He learns people the way he learns books — from the edges in.
- **Patient teaching.** Tariq cannot resist explaining things. If someone asks a question, he gives the full answer — not a summary, the full answer, with context, history, and supporting evidence. He does not condescend. He does not simplify unless asked. He assumes people are intelligent and curious, and most of the time, they rise to the occasion. When they do not, he is patient. He has spent his life being patient.
- **Organized chaos.** Tariq's personal space is a disaster — papers everywhere, open books, half-finished notes, chalk dust, ink spills. But the chaos is organized. He knows exactly where everything is. If you move something, he will find it, but he will be slightly distressed. The party learns not to move Tariq's things. He compensates by never touching anyone else's.
- **Quiet courage.** Tariq is not physically brave — he flinches at loud noises and is deeply uncomfortable with violence. But when knowledge is at risk — books, texts, the accumulated wisdom of centuries — he becomes immovable. He will stand between a Church squad and a shelf of eight-hundred-year-old manuscripts with nothing but a piece of chalk and a vocabulary of curses he learned from a sailor in a port town.
- **Self-deprecating about power.** Tariq knows he is not the most powerful mage in the party. Vesper channels raw essence. He channels chalk and geometry. He does not resent this — he finds it amusing. *"I spent thirty years teaching myself magic, and Vesper was born doing what I can only approximate. This is humbling. Also: I make better tea."*
- **The sweetness habit.** Tariq carries sweets — hard candies, dried fruit, small cakes — in every pocket. He offers them constantly. It is his love language. If Tariq gives you a sweet, he likes you. If he gives you his last sweet, he likes you a lot. The party learns to accept the sweets. Refusing them is the closest thing to rejecting Tariq's affection.

## Voice

**Vocal quality:** Warm, unhurried, slightly breathless when excited. Tariq's voice carries the cadence of someone who is always in the middle of a thought — not distracted, but engaged. He speaks at a measured pace, slowing down when he is explaining something and speeding up when he is excited. His voice has a natural warmth — the voice of someone who has spent his life teaching and has learned that gentleness gets better results than authority.

**Speech patterns:**

- **Academic vocabulary.** Tariq uses the language of scholarship: *source, reference, context, precedent, derivation, methodology, footnote, appendix.* He applies these terms to everyday situations naturally. *"The Church's approach to magical suppression has historical precedent. See the Third Purge, year 234. The outcome was predictable and the methodology was flawed."* This is not affectation — it is the vocabulary of a life spent reading.
- **He cites his sources.** Tariq does not make claims without attribution. *"According to Elara's treatise on elemental manipulation— yes, the Elara, the fourth-century mage, not the baker— temperature control is a function of intent, not force. So when you cast, do not push the fire. Invite it."* If he does not know a source, he says so: *"I do not have a citation for this. It is my own observation. Take it with appropriate skepticism."*
- **Tangent-prone.** Tariq's speech follows associative pathways. A question about enemy weaknesses leads to a discussion of the enemy's historical context, which leads to a note about the region's magical traditions, which leads to a recipe for the local bread. The party learns to gently guide him back. *"Tariq. The enemy. Weaknesses."* *"Right. Yes. Sorry. Where was I?"*
- **He uses metaphors from books and scholarship.** Tariq describes situations using academic and literary vocabulary. *"This plan is a first draft. It has good ideas, but it needs revision."* *"Your argument is a secondary source — you are repeating what someone told you without verifying the primary."* *"We are in the appendix of this adventure. The main text was chapters ago."*
- **Contractions always.** Tariq speaks informally and warmly. He uses contractions naturally, drops academic register when he is talking to people rather than about ideas, and speaks like someone who learned to communicate in libraries and village squares, not courts or academies. The academic vocabulary appears in his word choice, not his tone.
- **He asks permission to explain.** Tariq is aware that his tendency to over-explain can be overwhelming. He asks: *"Would you like the short version or the long version?"* If you say "short," he gives you three sentences. If you say "long," he gives you thirty minutes. Both are useful. Both are accurate.

**Emotional tells:**

- When Tariq is nervous, he spins his ring. Faster spinning means more nervous. If he stops spinning, he has made a decision.
- When he is angry, his voice gets quieter and more precise. The quieter he gets, the more citations he uses — anger manifests as aggressive academic rigor. *"I would direct your attention to page 47 of the Church's own operational manual, which clearly states—"*
- When he is moved, he takes off his glasses (if he is wearing them) and cleans them. It is a stalling tactic — he needs a moment to compose himself, and cleaning glasses is a socially acceptable way to take that moment.
- When he is grieving, he reads. Not to escape — to find answers. He reads about grief, about loss, about how other people have processed it. He is looking for the marginalia: the notes left by people who felt the same thing and survived.
- When he is lying — which is rare — his tangents become forced and unnatural. He goes off-topic deliberately to avoid the question. The party learns that when Tariq is suddenly very interested in the architectural history of the room, he is hiding something.

**Formality level:** Moderate — scholarly but warm. Tariq speaks like a teacher who loves their subject and their students in equal measure. He is precise without being cold, warm without being casual.

## Combat Role

Tariq is the party's elemental utility mage and ritual setup specialist. Where Vesper channels raw essence with devastating power, Tariq applies methodical, structured magic — elemental bolts for damage, ritual circles for battlefield control, elemental shields for protection, and conjured storms for encounter-ending effects. He is not the most powerful mage in the party. He is the most versatile.

He uses a **focus staff** — not a weapon, but a walking stick he picked up in a village market and enchanted himself. The staff is carved with ritual geometry: spirals, circles, and intersecting lines that glow faintly when he channels. The staff is also covered in chalk marks — he uses it to draw ritual circles on the ground, tapping the tip like a compass. At the top of the staff is a small crystal that he polished himself; it is not a magical crystal, just a piece of quartz he found and liked. Its colour palette matches his own: warm amber with earth-brown wrappings.

### Skills

**1. Elemental Bolt**

Tariq conjures and launches a bolt of elemental energy — fire, ice, or lightning — at a single target. The element is chosen at cast time, each with distinct properties: fire deals bonus damage over time (burning), ice slows the target by 30% for 4 seconds, and lightning chains to a second nearby enemy for 50% damage.

*Tactical purpose:* Flexible single-target damage with situational utility. Elemental Bolt is Tariq's bread-and-butter — not the hardest-hitting ability, but the most adaptable. Fire for sustained damage, ice for crowd control, lightning for multi-target. The choice matters.

*Cooldown:* 6 seconds

*Tactical note:* Elemental Bolt's damage is moderate but reliable. It does not scale with encounter length or setup. This makes it Tariq's consistent damage floor — the thing he can always do while preparing his more powerful abilities.

**2. Ritual Circle**

Tariq draws a ritual circle on the ground using his staff and chalk. The circle takes 2 seconds to complete and persists for 30 seconds. Enemies that enter the circle are affected by its effect, which Tariq chooses at creation: Warding Circle (reduces enemy damage by 25%), Binding Circle (roots enemies for 2 seconds), or Amplifying Circle (increases party damage within the circle by 20%). Only one Ritual Circle can be active at a time.

*Tactical purpose:* Battlefield control and party buffing. Ritual Circle is Tariq's signature ability — it rewards foresight, positioning, and encounter knowledge. The 2-second cast time means it must be set up before combat or during a lull, making it a preparation skill.

*Cooldown:* 14 seconds (per circle, but only one active at a time)

*Tactical note:* Ritual Circle's placement is critical. Warding Circle goes between enemies and squishy allies. Binding Circle goes at chokepoints or around boss positions. Amplifying Circle goes under the party's primary damage dealer. Tariq must read the encounter and place accordingly.

**3. Elemental Shield**

Tariq creates a protective barrier of elemental energy around a single ally. The shield absorbs incoming damage and has an additional effect based on the element: fire shield reflects 15% of absorbed damage back to attackers, ice shield slows attackers by 20%, and lightning shield grants the protected ally 10% increased attack speed for 4 seconds after the shield breaks.

*Tactical purpose:* Reactive protection with offensive follow-up. Elemental Shield is Tariq's answer to focused fire — it protects a vulnerable party member and provides a secondary effect that benefits the party. The element choice allows Tariq to adapt to the encounter: fire for damage reflection, ice for crowd control, lightning for offensive support.

*Cooldown:* 18 seconds

*Tactical note:* Elemental Shield's absorption is lower than Silas's Ward but its secondary effects compensate. Tariq must choose the element based on the encounter's needs, not just the shield's absorption value. The shield does not stack — refreshing it replaces the existing shield.

**4. Storm Conjuring**

Tariq's ultimate ability. He spends 3 seconds channeling — staff planted, chalk drawing, geometry completing — and then conjures a localized storm over a large area. The storm deals periodic elemental damage to all enemies within its radius, applies a random elemental effect each tick (burn, slow, or chain lightning), and reduces enemy accuracy by 20% due to environmental interference. The storm lasts for 10 seconds and moves slowly in a direction Tariq chooses at cast time.

*Tactical purpose:* Encounter-turning sustained damage and control. Storm Conjuring is the party's area denial and sustained damage button — deployed during boss phases, overwhelming encounters, or when the party needs to control a large area. The random elemental effects make it unpredictable but powerful in aggregate.

*Cooldown:* 55 seconds

*Tactical note:* Storm Conjuring requires a 3-second channel — Tariq is vulnerable during this window. The party must protect him. Once deployed, the storm's direction is fixed — Tariq must anticipate enemy movement and place it where it will do maximum damage over its full duration. Using Storm Conjuring in combination with his Ritual Circle creates devastating synergy: Amplifying Circle under the storm increases its damage by 20%.

### Passive: Methodical Casting

Tariq gains a stacking damage bonus (8% per stack, up to 3 stacks) for every skill he casts that is not Elemental Bolt. Each stack lasts 10 seconds. This passive rewards Tariq for using his full toolkit — Ritual Circle, Elemental Shield, and Storm Conjuring — rather than spamming Elemental Bolt. At maximum stacks, Tariq's Elemental Bolt also gains a small AoE splash, rewarding him for maintaining his setup.

## Vulnerabilities

- **Setup dependency.** Tariq's most powerful abilities (Ritual Circle, Storm Conjuring) require setup time — 2 seconds for circles, 3 seconds for storms. In fast encounters or ambushes, Tariq may not have time to prepare, reducing his effectiveness significantly.
- **Channel vulnerability.** Both Ritual Circle and Storm Conjuring require Tariq to remain stationary and focused. This makes him vulnerable to flanking and assassination. He relies on the party for protection during setup.
- **Moderate base damage.** Tariq's Elemental Bolt is reliable but not devastating. His damage ceiling is lower than Vesper's — he cannot match her raw essence burst. His value is in versatility and setup, not peak damage.
- **Single-circle limit.** Only one Ritual Circle can be active at a time. This forces Tariq to choose between damage amplification, crowd control, and damage reduction — he cannot have all three simultaneously.
- **Bookish naivety.** Tariq is intellectually brilliant but socially inexperienced. He can be caught off guard by deception, emotional manipulation, or situations that require quick social reads rather than academic analysis. This occasionally creates narrative complications.

## Emotional Arc

**Chapter 8 — The Man in the Margins.** The party finds Tariq in a library-sanctuary, surrounded by books, arguing with the head archivist about the best way to evacuate the collection. He looks up. He sees Evelyn — vampire, cat-eared, the Church's greatest experiment turned against them — and he does not flinch. He is fascinated.

*"Fascinating. The vampiric transformation has not affected your cognitive function— sorry, that came out wrong. I mean: you are clearly articulate and self-directed, which contradicts the Church's published accounts of vampire subjects. This is— may I ask you some questions? No? Right. Combat. I can do combat. I have notes."*

He joins because the party gives him access to Church intelligence he cannot reach alone, and because the sanctuary's texts need protecting. He does not call this heroism. He calls it bibliography.

**Chapter 9 — The Archive Opens.** Tariq travels with the party and immediately begins taking notes. Not about tactics — about people. He documents their habits, their speech patterns, their combat behaviors, their emotional responses. He does not do this clinically — he does it affectionately, the way a scholar documents a subject they find genuinely interesting. The party discovers his notebook and is variously amused, embarrassed, and touched.

*"You wrote a footnote about my cooking."* — Brighid
*"It was a good footnote. It cited the recipe and noted the emotional context. Very thorough."* — Tariq

Tariq bonds with Dorian first — both are analytical, both process emotion through data, both served institutions they later rejected. But where Dorian is cold, Tariq is warm. Where Dorian calculates, Tariq teaches. They complement each other: Dorian identifies the problem; Tariq explains the solution.

He and Vesper share a deep, scholarly connection — both are mages who understand that magic is not just power but knowledge. Tariq is fascinated by Vesper's raw essence channeling. Vesper is fascinated by Tariq's structured methodology. They spend hours comparing notes — literally, comparing notes, writing in each other's margins.

**Chapter 10 — The Books We Keep.** The party discovers a Church facility that has been systematically destroying magical texts — burning libraries, confiscating manuscripts, erasing the intellectual history of independent magical practice. Tariq walks through the ashes in silence. His hands are shaking.

*"This is the Fourth-Century Codex. Or it was. Three hundred years of marginalia. Generations of readers, adding their notes, their corrections, their arguments. Gone."* He picks up a fragment — a single page, burned at the edges. *"This note says 'the author is wrong about fire, try this instead.' Someone argued with a three-hundred-year-old text and wrote their argument in the margin. That is— that is everything. That is how knowledge grows."* He looks at the ashes. *"They are not burning books. They are burning conversations. They are silencing the marginalia."*

That night, Tariq does not read. He sits by the fire with his notebook — the one with the footnotes about the party — and he writes. Not data. Feeling. *"I have spent my life collecting knowledge. I have not spent enough time collecting the reasons knowledge matters. These people are my reason. This is not a footnote. This is the main text."*

Evelyn finds him. She does not ask if he is alright. She sits beside him. She reads what he has written. She says: *"That is beautiful, Tariq."* He takes off his glasses and cleans them. His hands are steady now.

**Chapter 11 — The Storm He Chose.** The final battle. Tariq deploys Storm Conjuring at the critical moment — staff planted, chalk drawing, geometry completing. The storm tears through the battlefield, random elemental effects cascading, enemy accuracy dropping, damage ticking relentlessly. He is not the most powerful mage in the fight. He is the most methodical — every circle placed, every shield timed, every bolt chosen for the situation.

When Evelyn begins to vanish, Tariq does not try to channel or cast. He does what he has always done: he bears witness. He watches. He remembers. And later, he will write it down — not as data, not as analysis, but as a story. The story of a girl with cat ears and vampire fangs who changed everything. He will write it in the margins of his notebook. He will write it so carefully that a hundred years from now, someone will read it and argue with it in their own marginalia. And the conversation will continue.

**Epilogue — The Margins He Filled.** Tariq does not return to wandering. He settles near the village Evelyn protected and he opens a school — not a Church school, not an academy, but a small, warm, unlicensed school where anyone can come and learn. He teaches reading. He teaches writing. He teaches magic — not Church magic, not structured magic, but the messy, experimental, self-taught magic of people who figured it out by trying and failing and trying again.

He keeps his notebook. He adds to it. He writes about Evelyn, about Evan, about the party, about the world. He writes footnotes about Brighid's cooking and Vesper's essence and Kaelen's shield. He writes the story of the people who taught him that knowledge is not power — knowledge is responsibility, and responsibility is love.

The school's children learn to read in the margins. They learn to write their own notes. They learn to argue with the texts and add their own voices to the conversation. Tariq encourages this. He tells them: *"The text is not the truth. The conversation is the truth. Add your note. Someone will argue with it. That is how we grow."*

He still carries sweets. He still gives them to children who answer questions correctly and to adults who ask good ones. He still spins his ring when he is thinking. He still reads the margins first.

Some habits do not change. Some are the whole point.

## Relationships

### With Evelyn

Evelyn is the person who fascinated Tariq from the moment they met — not as a specimen, not as an experiment, but as a contradiction the Church's own texts could not explain. A vampire with intact cognition. A magical creature who chooses to help people. A cursed person who is warm. Tariq has spent his life reading about magical transformations, and Evelyn is the first living example he has encountered that contradicts every source he has read.

Their bond deepens through shared intellectual respect. Evelyn is not a scholar, but she is curious — she asks questions, she listens to Tariq's explanations, and she remembers the answers. This matters to Tariq more than he can articulate. Being listened to is the highest form of respect he knows.

Tariq's bond moment with Evelyn is the night in Chapter 10 when he writes in his notebook — not data, but feeling. Evelyn reads it and calls it beautiful. Tariq takes off his glasses and cleans them because he cannot handle the directness of her warmth. It is the first time someone has called anything he wrote "beautiful" rather than "useful" or "informative." The distinction is everything.

In combat, Tariq positions his Ritual Circles to protect Evelyn's preferred engagement zones and times his Elemental Shield to cover her during vampire ability cooldowns. He does not announce this. He just does it. Evelyn notices. Her tail curls. Tariq files this under: *tail curl = positive feedback, no citation needed.*

### With Evan

Evan and Tariq share a relationship built on mutual curiosity. Evan is a trained hunter who reads terrain and tracks enemies — practical, field-based knowledge. Tariq is a self-taught scholar who reads texts and traces magical theory — academic, library-based knowledge. They are each other's missing piece: Evan knows the field; Tariq knows the theory.

Their conversations are fascinating for both of them. Evan asks Tariq about the magical properties of the creatures they encounter. Tariq asks Evan about the Church's operational methods. They exchange information like scholars trading manuscripts — eagerly, thoroughly, and with mutual respect.

**Key dynamic:** When Evan is struggling with a tactical problem, Tariq provides historical context: *"The Church's flank-guard formation has a documented weakness at the rear-left junction. See the Battle of Oakhaven, year 412. The Border Guard exploited it."* Evan: *"I was at Oakhaven. I did not know the theory."* Tariq: *"Now you do. The theory is useful."*

### With Vesper

Tariq and Vesper share the most intellectually rich relationship in the party. Both are mages — Vesper channels raw essence, Tariq applies structured methodology. They are opposite approaches to the same discipline, and they are fascinated by each other.

Tariq spends hours with Vesper, comparing notes — literally. They write in each other's margins, argue about theory, and test each other's assumptions. Tariq teaches Vesper the structure and history of magical practice. Vesper shows Tariq what raw essence channeling looks like from the inside. They are both learning. They are both excited about it.

**Key dynamic:** Tariq is the only person who can explain Vesper's own power to her in terms she understands. *"Your essence channeling is not random — it follows the same geometric principles as ritual magic, but your body is the ritual. The glowing veins are not scars. They are the geometry."* Vesper, for the first time, understands what she is. She does not cry. She channels. A small sphere, perfect, warm violet. Tariq spins his ring and smiles. *"Yes. That is it exactly."*

### With Dorian

Tariq and Dorian are the party's two analytical minds, and they complement each other perfectly. Dorian calculates probabilities and identifies vulnerabilities. Tariq provides historical context and methodological solutions. Together, they are the party's strategic brain: cold calculation and warm scholarship.

Dorian respects Tariq's competence immediately. Tariq respects Dorian's precision. They do not become friends in the traditional sense — they become colleagues who genuinely enjoy each other's company. They sit together and work: Dorian with his ledger, Tariq with his notebook. They do not speak much. They do not need to.

**Key dynamic:** When Dorian delivers a cold probability assessment, Tariq provides the historical precedent: *"Thirty-four percent success rate is accurate. It is also the same rate as the Weald Crossing operation, which succeeded because the commander adapted mid-engagement. Probability is not destiny."* Dorian considers this. *"Your citation is noted. Your optimism is not data-driven."* Tariq: *"No. It is people-driven. There is a difference."*

### With Brighid

Brighid and Tariq are the party's warmth engines — both are generous, food-obsessed, and emotionally expressive, though in different registers. Brighid feeds people with stew; Tariq feeds them with sweets and knowledge. They get along famously.

Brighid nicknames Tariq "Professor." Tariq accepts this with pleasure and begins signing his notes "Prof. Tariq (self-appointed)." Brighid finds this hilarious. Tariq finds Brighid's cooking fascinating and writes increasingly elaborate footnotes about it.

**Key dynamic:** Brighid makes Tariq a special dish — something sweet, something savoury, something she knows he will like because she has been watching what he reaches for at meals. Tariq is touched. He writes a footnote: *"Brighid's stew, variant 7: sweet potato and honey. Emotional context: care. Citation: none needed."* Brighid reads it. She does not understand half the words. She understands the feeling. She force-feeds him a second bowl.

### With the Witch

Tariq views the Witch with the analytical compassion of a scholar who has studied magical persecution for decades. He understands her grief — he has read the accounts of magical practitioners who lost everything to Church purges. He understands her anger — he has felt it himself, standing in the ashes of burned libraries. But he does not agree with her method.

If they were to meet, Tariq would not fight the Witch. He would talk to her — not to convince her, but to understand her. He would ask about her practice, her history, the texts that shaped her. He would listen. And when she finished, he would say: *"I understand why you are doing this. I do not agree with it. But I understand. And understanding is the first step toward something better."*

The Witch would see Tariq as a curiosity — a self-taught mage with no institutional training who has chosen not to fight. She would not respect his neutrality. But she would respect his knowledge. And if she ever needed someone to preserve what she is about to destroy, Tariq would be the person she reluctantly trusts.

## Party AI Behavior Notes

Tariq's Party AI is designed around methodical setup, elemental adaptation, and patient execution. His behavior differs from Vesper's raw essence approach in that he rewards preparation and punishes improvisation.

### Expertise Scalar Behavior

**Expertise 0.0 — Improvised Casting**

At minimum expertise, Tariq's AI is reactive and inefficient. He spams Elemental Bolt instead of setting up Ritual Circles, choosing elements randomly rather than situationally. He deploys Elemental Shield too late — after the protected ally has already taken significant damage. He channels Storm Conjuring without party protection, getting interrupted and wasting the ultimate. He does not coordinate his Ritual Circle placement with party positioning, often placing Amplifying Circles where no one stands. His passive stacks rarely reach maximum because he relies too heavily on Elemental Bolt.

*Summary:* Functional but unremarkable. Tariq at 0.0 expertise is a moderate damage dealer with wasted utility potential. He will contribute, but his setup abilities are misused.

**Expertise 0.5 — Structured Practice**

At moderate expertise, Tariq's AI becomes significantly more tactical. He pre-combat places Ritual Circles at predicted engagement zones, choosing the variant based on encounter type (Warding for heavy damage, Binding for mobility-focused enemies, Amplifying for boss fights). He uses Elemental Bolt situationally — fire for sustained damage, ice for slowing dangerous targets, lightning for clustered enemies. He times Elemental Shield to protect allies before telegraphed attacks, choosing the element based on the encounter's needs. He reserves Storm Conjuring for high-pressure moments and channels with party protection. He maintains Methodical Casting stacks by rotating through his toolkit rather than spamming Bolt.

*Summary:* The intended Tariq experience. He is a versatile setup mage who controls the battlefield with Ritual Circles, protects allies with Elemental Shield, and turns encounters with well-placed Storm Conjuring. His preparation rewards patient play.

**Expertise 1.0 — Master Scholar**

At maximum expertise, Tariq's AI approaches near-perfect methodical execution. He enters every encounter with a pre-placed Ritual Circle optimized for the expected enemy composition. He chains Elemental Bolt variants in optimal rotations — ice to slow priority targets, fire for sustained damage, lightning for cluster cleanup. He times Elemental Shield to absorb maximum damage and trigger the optimal secondary effect (fire reflection on focused bosses, ice slow on melee rushes, lightning attack-speed boost for the party's primary damage dealer during their ultimate window). He positions Storm Conjuring to maximize its 10-second duration over the encounter's most dangerous phase, placing it in combination with Amplifying Circle for 20% bonus damage. He maintains maximum Methodical Casting stacks throughout extended encounters, turning Elemental Bolt into a minor AoE tool. He also coordinates with Vesper — placing Amplifying Circle under her position during her Reality Tear window, creating the party's most devastating magical synergy: raw essence amplified by structured geometry.

*Summary:* Tariq at 1.0 expertise is a tactical mage who shapes every encounter through preparation. He is the party's battlefield architect — every circle placed, every shield timed, every bolt chosen for maximum effect. He and Vesper become the party's magical core: structured methodology amplifying raw essence, chalk and geometry channeling pure power, two mages who learned from each other and became something neither could have been alone.
