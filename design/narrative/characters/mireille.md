# Mireille

**Role**: Party Support — The Bard Who Sang for the Dead
**POV Chapters**: Joins Chapter 5, full presence Chapters 6 through 11
**Visual**: Warm gold and faded rose palette. Petite and expressive, with the animated physicality of someone who has spent her life performing for audiences that range from enthusiastic to hostile. Her skin is sun-warmed brown, dusted with freckles across her nose that she does not bother to hide. Her hair is a thick mane of copper-red curls, half-tied back with a faded ribbon that has been re-tied so many times the fabric is fraying — a deliberate choice, not neglect. Her eyes are bright hazel, flecked with gold, and extraordinarily mobile: they telegraph every emotion before her face catches up. She wears layered fabrics in a festival singer's aesthetic — a fitted bodice with embroidered floral patterns, a flowing skirt split for movement, a short capelet pinned at the shoulder with a small brass pin shaped like a lute. Her fingers are calloused from strings. She carries a **viol** — a curved, handheld string instrument that doubles as her magical focus and her weapon. The viol is scarred: a crack running along its lower bout that has been carefully glued and reinforced, worn tuning pegs, strings that glow faintly when she channels. Her silhouette is unmistakable: the instrument always present, the capelet catching the wind, the bounce in her step that never fully disappears even on the worst days.

## Backstory

Mireille was born in a village called Rosethorne — a small, unremarkable farming settlement in the lowlands that was known for exactly two things: its midsummer festival and its music. The festival was the bigger draw. Every year, on the longest night, the entire village gathered in the central square and sang from dusk until dawn. Not performed — sang. Everyone participated, from toddlers to elders, and the music was the kind of living, breathing thing that no single person owned. Mireille's mother led the singing. Her father played the fiddle. Mireille learned the viol before she could read, and by the time she was ten she was accompanying her mother's lead vocals with harmonies that made old women cry.

The midsummer festival was not entertainment. It was the village's identity — the one thing that made Rosethorne more than a collection of farms. It was where courtships were announced, where grievances were forgiven, where the dead were remembered with songs instead of silence. It was the village's heart.

The Church came during the festival.

It was not a purge — it was a "containment operation." The Church had detected magical energy emanating from Rosethorne and classified it as an unlicensed ritual — a gathering of people channeling collective emotional energy without Church oversight. The Inquisition detachment arrived at midnight, during the height of the singing. They did not ask the village to stop. They declared the ritual a threat, sealed the square with blessed barriers, and began "dispersing" the crowd.

Mireille was fourteen. She was playing the viol when the barriers went up. She watched a Church soldier strike her father with the flat of a blessed blade for refusing to stop playing. She watched her mother try to shield him and get caught in the barrier's edge — the blessed energy searing her hands, the hands that had led the singing for thirty years. She watched neighbors — people she had sung with her entire life — treated as a magical threat for the crime of singing together.

When she tried to help her mother, a soldier grabbed her viol and raised it to smash it. Mireille screamed — not a word, just sound, raw and terrified and furious — and the soldier dropped the instrument. She does not know if it was the scream or something else. The viol has a crack in it from that night. She never fixed it completely.

The Church broke up the festival. They banned it. They classified the midsummer singing as an "Unlicensed Collective Emotional Ritual" and forbade its practice. Rosethorne's heart stopped beating. The village went quiet — not the comfortable quiet of rest, but the hollow quiet of something that has been taken.

Mireille left. She traveled from village to village, singing for food and shelter, performing at whatever gatherings people would still hold in secret. She became good at reading a room — knowing when to lift spirits, when to soothe, when to let silence do the work. Her music became her way of giving people what Rosethorne had lost: a reason to sing together, even if it had to be hidden, even if it had to be small.

She never went back to Rosethorne. She was afraid of what she would find — not destruction, but acceptance. The village that had learned to live without its heart.

She joins Evelyn's party in Chapter 5 when they find her performing at a refugee gathering in an abandoned barn. The refugees are displaced villagers, Church defectors, and people who have nowhere else to go. Mireille is singing — not a festival song, something quieter, something that makes people hold hands without thinking about it. Evelyn hears it from outside the barn. She stops. Her ears twitch. Her tail goes still. She has heard music like this before — in her own village, before the Church, before everything.

Mireille sees Evelyn at the door — vampire ears, cat tail, the whole thing — and her eyes go wide. Then she keeps playing. She finishes the song. She sets down her viol. She walks over and says: *"I have heard stories about you. Most of them are lies. The truth is better."*

She joins because Evelyn represents something she has not seen since Rosethorne: someone the world has hurt who still sings for other people. The parallel is exact and irresistible.

## Personality

**Core traits:** Warm, performative, deeply empathetic, carries private grief behind public joy, stubbornly communal.

Mireille is a person who processes the world through connection and sound. She reads rooms the way other people read books — tone, tempo, body language, the collective mood of a group as a single data point. She is good at making people feel seen, heard, and less alone. This is not a skill she learned; it is who she is. The stage taught her how to do it well.

**Behavioral patterns:**

- **Performance as social fluency.** Mireille defaults to warmth and humor in social situations. She is the person who breaks tension with a song, who turns an awkward silence into a shared laugh, who notices when someone is withdrawing and pulls them gently back into the group. This is not an act — it is her natural state, amplified by years of performing. But the amplification has a cost: when the performance drops, the grief underneath is visible.
- **Music as emotional language.** Mireille describes non-musical situations using musical vocabulary: *tempo, harmony, discord, rhythm, key, rest, crescendo, resolve.* *"This plan is all crescendo and no resolve. We need a rest before we break."* *"The party is out of tune. Not badly — just a half-step off. We will find our harmony."*
- **Private grief.** Mireille does not talk about Rosethorne. Not at first. She deflects with warmth and humor, and the deflection works because she is genuinely warm and genuinely funny. The party learns that when Mireille goes quiet — when she is not performing, not filling the space with sound — something has touched the wound. She will tune her viol very carefully. She will touch the crack in its wood. She will not speak until she is ready.
- **Communal instinct.** Mireille believes that people are better together than apart. This is not a philosophy — it is an observation, backed by every festival she has ever attended and every secret gathering she has ever sung at. She organizes camp singalongs without asking. She harmonizes with Ione's humming. She teaches Corin a rhythm to tap on his crossbow stock to calm his nerves. She builds community the way Corin builds perimeter traps: deliberately, skillfully, because it keeps people alive.
- **Festival singer aesthetic as armor.** Mireille dresses like she is always ready to perform — bright colors, flowing fabrics, the capelet, the brass lute pin. This is deliberate. The aesthetic says "I am a performer, I am here to entertain you, do not look too closely." It works on most people. It does not work on Evelyn, who recognizes a fellow deflector.
- **Fierce protectiveness of music.** If someone tries to silence music — literally or metaphorically — Mireille's warmth becomes steel. She does not raise her voice. She plays louder. *"You banned a song? You do not get to ban a song. Songs are not yours to ban."*

## Voice

**Vocal quality:** Bright, resonant, naturally musical. Mireille's voice carries the warmth of someone who has sung for crowds and learned to project without strain. When she speaks normally, her voice has a singing quality — not affected, but inherent, the way some people's voices ring. When she is emotional, her voice does not crack; it goes quiet and very precise, as if she is choosing each note.

**Speech patterns:**

- **Musical metaphors.** Mireille uses the language of music naturally: *tempo, harmony, discord, rhythm, key, rest, crescendo, resolve, bridge, refrain, chorus.* *"That plan is all crescendo and no resolve. We need a rest."* *"The Church is a discordant note. You do not harmonize with discord — you resolve it or you stop singing."*
- **Warm directness.** Mireille speaks warmly but directly. She does not hedge or flatter. She tells people what she sees with the same kindness she would use to sing them a lullaby. *"You are carrying something heavy, Evan. Put it down for a minute. The world will not end in sixty seconds."*
- **She uses people's names like melody notes.** Mireille says names with intention — stretching syllables, softening consonants, making each name sound like it belongs in a song. *"Eve-lyn."* *"Cor-in."* *"Sil-as."* People notice. They do not always know why it feels good to hear their name from her.
- **Performance voice vs. real voice.** Mireille has two registers: her "stage voice" (bright, projecting, slightly larger than life) and her "real voice" (softer, slower, more careful). The party learns to recognize the shift. When she drops the stage voice, something matters.
- **She sings fragments when thinking.** Mireille hums or sings half-remembered melodies when she is processing information — not full songs, just a bar or two, the way some people tap their fingers. The melodies are old — festival songs, lullabies, things from Rosethorne. She does not realize she is doing it until someone asks what she is singing. Then she stops. Then, later, she starts again.
- **Rare fury.** When Mireille is truly angry — when someone tries to silence music, or when the Church's cruelty toward ordinary people is on display — her voice loses its warmth entirely. It becomes flat, precise, and cutting. *"You took a village's song and called it a threat. You do not get to do that. You do not get to take something people love and rename it as danger because you do not understand it."*
- **She does not do silence well.** Mireille fills silence with sound — humming, tuning, tapping, singing. When she cannot fill it (grief, shock, fear), she becomes visibly uncomfortable. The party learns that Mireille in genuine silence means Mireille in genuine pain.

**Emotional tells:**

- When Mireille is happy, her tempo increases — she speaks faster, moves quicker, her fingers drum rhythms on whatever surface is nearest.
- When she is grieving, she goes very still and very quiet. She touches the crack in her viol. She does not play. The absence of music is the tell.
- When she is nervous, she tunes her viol obsessively — even when it is already in tune. The tuning pegs become a fidget tool.
- When she is genuinely moved, her eyes go gold-bright and she looks away. She does not like people seeing her cry. She will pretend to adjust a string until the moment passes.
- When she is lying, she performs harder — brighter smile, bigger voice, more energy. Over-performance is her tell. The party learns that when Mireille is "great!" and "wonderful!" with too much enthusiasm, she is not okay.

**Formality level:** Low. Mireille speaks like someone who learned to communicate in villages and at festivals, not courts or academies. She is warm, expressive, and entirely unguarded — until she is very guarded, and then the contrast is striking.

## Combat Role

Mireille is the party's morale engine and tempo controller. Where Silas heals bodies and Ione heals ecosystems, Mireille heals *momentum* — she controls the pace of combat, amplifies party effectiveness, and disrupts enemy coordination through musical buffs and debuffs. She is not a primary healer or a damage dealer. She is the person who makes everyone else better at their job.

She channels music magic through her **viol** — a curved string instrument that functions as both weapon and focus. When she plays, the music creates visible wave patterns in the air: golden harmonics for buffs, dissonant dark ripples for debuffs. The crack in the viol glows when she channels at full power, a reminder of the night it was almost destroyed.

### Skills

**1. Rallying Chorus**

Mireille plays an uplifting melody that grants all party members within a moderate radius a stacking attack and speed buff for a short duration. Each stack increases damage output by 8% and movement speed by 3%, up to 4 stacks. Stacks decay one at a time when the buff expires.

*Gameplay purpose:* Core offensive buff. Rallying Chorus is the party's tempo button — deployed before engagement or during push phases to maximize damage windows. The stacking mechanic rewards sustained presence in the buff zone, encouraging party positioning.

*Tactical note:* Mireille cannot move while channeling Rallying Chorus. She plants her feet and plays, vulnerable but powerful. The party must protect her during the channel.

**2. Dirge of the Fallen**

Mireille plays a mournful, dissonant melody that applies a stacking debuff to all enemies in a cone in front of her. Each stack reduces enemy attack speed by 5% and accuracy by 4%, up to 5 stacks. Enemies with maximum stacks have a chance to skip their next action entirely as they are overwhelmed by the weight of the music.

*Gameplay purpose:* Enemy disruption and tempo control. Dirge of the Fallen slows dangerous enemy phases, reduces incoming damage through accuracy debuffs, and occasionally shuts down enemies completely. It is most effective against groups of mid-tier enemies rather than single bosses.

*Tactical note:* Dirge is a cone, not an AoE. Mireille must face the enemy group and maintain the channel for maximum effect. Positioning matters.

**3. Bridge of Hearts**

Mireille plays a connecting melody that links two party members with a visible golden thread for a moderate duration. Linked members share 30% of incoming damage with each other (reducing individual spikes) and gain a small health regeneration aura. If one linked member falls below 25% health, the other gains a brief damage and defense boost.

*Gameplay purpose:* Damage smoothing and pair synergy. Bridge of Hearts is deployed on two party members who are taking focused fire, turning lethal spikes into manageable sustained damage. The low-health trigger reward encourages strategic pairing — linking a tank with a squishy creates a safety net, while linking two damage dealers creates a burst window.

*Tactical note:* Only one Bridge can be active at a time. Mireille must choose the pairing carefully based on the encounter.

**4. Festival of the Last Light (Ultimate)**

Mireille plays the song Rosethorne sang on the last night before the Church came — the midsummer festival anthem, the one she thought she would never play again. For a moderate duration, the entire party gains massive buffs: all stats increased by 25%, all cooldowns reduced by 30%, and a persistent AoE healing field centered on Mireille that restores health and removes debuffs. Additionally, enemies within the field take periodic psychic damage and are forced to attack Mireille's current target (forced focus).

*Gameplay purpose:* Encounter-defining tempo shift. Festival of the Last Light is the party's "everything changes now" button — deployed during boss enrage phases, overwhelming ambushes, or when the party needs to push through an encounter that is otherwise unsustainable. The forced focus on Mireille's target means the entire party can burst a single enemy with maximum efficiency.

*Tactical note:* Festival costs Mireille significant emotional energy. After it ends, she is depleted — her buffs are weaker and her energy regenerates more slowly for a short window. The party must protect her during the recovery phase. Using Festival is a commitment, not a casual play.

### Passive: Conductor's Ear

Mireille gains increased buff duration for each unique party member currently alive and active. With a full party, her buffs last 20% longer. As party members fall, the duration decreases. This passive reinforces Mireille's identity as a communal performer — she is strongest when everyone is present and fighting together.

### Vulnerabilities

- **Channel dependency.** Most of Mireille's skills require her to channel — she cannot move while playing. This makes her vulnerable to flanking, assassination, and area attacks. The party must position to protect her.
- **Low personal damage.** Mireille's combat effectiveness is almost entirely supportive. Her viol can deal minor physical damage in emergencies, but she relies entirely on the party to defeat enemies.
- **Emotional resonance cost.** Festival of the Last Light and heavy buff usage drain Mireille's emotional energy, not just her combat energy. After extended buff deployment, her performance degrades until she recovers. This creates meaningful resource management.
- **Silence vulnerability.** Enemies with silence abilities completely shut down Mireille's toolkit. She has no non-musical fallback. Silence is her hard counter and appears in late-game Church encounters.
- **Conductor's Ear inverse.** As party members fall, Mireille's buffs become shorter and less effective. In desperate situations where the party is dwindling, Mireille becomes less able to turn the tide — a deliberate design choice that reinforces the importance of keeping the party alive.

## Emotional Arc

**Chapter 5 — The Song in the Barn.** The party finds Mireille performing at a refugee gathering in an abandoned barn. She is warm, animated, keeping spirits up with music that makes people hold hands without thinking about it. Evelyn hears it from outside. She stops. Her ears twitch. Her tail goes still.

Mireille sees Evelyn and keeps playing. She finishes the song. She sets down her viol. She approaches with wide hazel eyes and says: *"I have heard stories about you. Most of them are lies. The truth is better."* She joins because Evelyn is someone the world has hurt who still helps people — the same pattern as Mireille herself, and she cannot resist it.

**Chapters 6 through 7 — The Harmony Forms.** Mireille integrates with the party quickly — she is a joiner by nature, and the party is the first group she has traveled with that feels like a community rather than a collection of strangers. She organizes camp singalongs. She harmonizes with Ione's humming. She teaches Evan a drinking song from Rosethorne (he does not drink, but he learns the melody). She is in her element.

She does not talk about Rosethorne. Not yet. When the topic of home comes up, she deflects with a joke or a song. The party accepts this — for now.

**Chapters 7 through 8 — The Crack in the Viol.** The party passes near Rosethorne. Mireille knows — she has been tracking their route, and she realizes where they are. She does not mention it. But her performance that night is different: brighter, louder, more forced. She is performing to cover something, and the party notices.

Evelyn finds her alone after the singing stops, tuning her viol for the third time in ten minutes. The tuning pegs are trembling in her hands.

*"We are near Rosethorne. My village. I have not been back since the Church came. They broke up our midsummer festival — called it a ritual, called it a threat. My mother's hands were burned. My father was struck. I was fourteen and I could not —"* Her voice cracks. She stops. She touches the crack in the viol. *"I have never gone back. I am afraid the village learned to live without the music. I am afraid I would hear silence where there used to be singing."*

Evelyn does not offer reassurance. She offers presence. *"My village still sings. Not the same songs. But they sing. Sometimes the music changes. It does not mean it is gone."*

Mireille looks at her. Her eyes are gold-bright. She looks away. She plucks a single note on the viol. It is out of tune. She fixes it. *"Thank you, Evelyn."*

**Chapters 9 through 10 — The Song She Carries.** Mireille becomes the party's emotional tempo controller — not just in combat, but in camp, in travel, in the quiet moments between encounters. She notices when someone is withdrawing and pulls them back with a song, a joke, a question. She is the party's social glue, and she knows it.

She begins to talk about Rosethorne more openly — not the pain, but the joy. She describes the midsummer festival with the vivid specificity of someone who is reliving it: the smell of bonfire smoke, the taste of festival bread, the sound of two hundred voices singing the same melody under the same sky. The party listens. They are hearing a ghost story — not of death, but of something alive that was killed.

She teaches the party the midsummer song. Not all of it — just the chorus. They sing it together around a campfire, and Mireille cries without making a sound. Her eyes are gold. Her hands are steady. She plays the viol.

**Chapter 11 — The Last Song.** The final battle. Mireille deploys Festival of the Last Light — the midsummer anthem, the forbidden song, the one the Church tried to erase. She plays it in front of the Church's forces, loud and unapologetic, and the music hits enemies like a physical force. She is not buffing the party anymore. She is weaponizing joy.

When Evelyn begins to vanish, Mireille does something she has never done: she stops playing. She sets down her viol. She walks to Evelyn and takes her hand. *"I have a song for you. But not yet. Not yet. I will play it when I am ready, and you will hear it wherever you are."*

**Epilogue — The Festival That Returned.** Mireille goes back to Rosethorne. She does not know what she will find. She arrives on midsummer night — the longest night, the festival night — and she stands in the central square where it all happened. The square is empty. The bonfire pit is cold.

She takes out her viol. She plays the first note of the midsummer song.

A voice joins from the doorway — an old woman, one of the original singers, her hands scarred but her voice intact. Then another voice, from the next house. Then another. One by one, the villagers emerge and sing. Not the full festival — not yet. But enough. Enough to prove that the music was never gone. It was waiting.

Mireille plays until dawn. She does not cry. She smiles. The crack in her viol glows gold, and for the first time since she was fourteen, she is singing at home.

## Relationships

### With Evelyn

Mireille and Evelyn share the most immediate bond of anyone in the party. Both are women the Church hurt who chose to help people anyway. Both use warmth and service as their primary language of care. Both deflect pain with humor and performance — Evelyn with dry wit, Mireille with song. They recognize each other instantly.

Their bond deepens through mutual recognition of deflection. Evelyn sees through Mireille's performance; Mireille sees through Evelyn's humor. Neither pushes the other to open up. They wait. When they do — in Chapter 8, when Mireille finally talks about Rosethorne — the conversation is quiet, honest, and transformative.

Mireille's version of affection for Evelyn is musical. She writes a melody for Evelyn — not a song with words, just a tune on the viol that captures Evelyn's essence: warm, sad, determined, faster than it sounds. She plays it when Evelyn is nearby. Evelyn never asks what it is called. She does not need to.

**Bond moment:** In Chapter 8, when Mireille tells Evelyn about Rosethorne, Evelyn shares her own story about the village she protects in secret — the people who do not know she exists but would be dead without her. *"I cannot go home either. Not because it is gone. Because going back would put them in danger. I protect them by staying away."* Mireille plays a single note. *"That is the saddest kind of love. The kind that cannot be near the thing it loves."* Evelyn's tail curls. *"Yes. But it is still love."*

### With Evan

Evan and Mireille share a warm, slightly awkward relationship. Evan is not musical — he is practical, tactical, grounded. Mireille is the opposite. But they connect through their shared experience of institutional betrayal: Evan served the Church; Mireille was a victim of it. Both carry the weight of what the Church took from them.

Mireille teaches Evan a drinking song from Rosethorne. He does not drink, but he learns the melody. He cannot carry a tune, and Mireille finds this both hilarious and endearing. She teases him gently. He accepts it with the good humor of someone who knows he is musically hopeless but appreciates the effort.

Their combat coordination is functional but not instinctive. Evan tracks priority targets; Mireille buffs the party to focus them. They communicate through tactical shorthand rather than musical flow.

**Key dynamic:** When Evan spirals into self-doubt, Mireille does not offer tactical reassurance. She offers music — she plays something warm and steady and sits beside him until his breathing slows. *"You do not have to solve everything tonight, Evan. Just listen to this. That is your only job."* He listens. It helps.

### With Silas

Mireille and Silas share a warm, grounding relationship. Both are healers in different registers — Silas heals bodies, Mireille heals momentum. Both carry grief without performing it (though Mireille performs more than she admits). Both use service as their primary language.

They complement each other in combat: Silas keeps party members alive; Mireille keeps them effective. Silas's Ward + Mireille's Rallying Chorus creates a window where a party member is both protected and amplified — the party's most powerful single-target combination.

Off the battlefield, they share quiet moments. Silas listens to Mireille play and says little, but his stillness is approval. Mireille notices. She plays longer when Silas is listening.

**Key dynamic:** Silas is one of the first people to see through Mireille's performance. When she is over-performing — too bright, too loud, too much — Silas says gently: *"Mireille. You are playing fortissimo when the room needs piano. It is all right to be quiet."* She stops. She tunes her viol. She says: *"Thank you, Silas."*

### With Kaelen

Mireille and Kaelen share a warm, slightly bemused relationship. Kaelen is not musical, not performative, not expressive. Mireille is all of those things. She finds him fascinating. He finds her exhausting and wonderful in equal measure.

Mireille tries to teach Kaelen to hum. He cannot. He is rhythmically tone-deaf. She finds this hilarious and makes it her personal mission to teach him one note. After three chapters, he can produce a sound that is approximately a C. She declares victory. He is embarrassed and secretly pleased.

In combat, Mireille buffs Kaelen with Rallying Chorus, and Kaelen becomes an immovable force with amplified threat. She calls it "making the wall louder." He does not know how to respond to this. He accepts the buff and holds the line.

**Key dynamic:** Mireille once played the midsummer song near Kaelen, and he listened without speaking. When she finished, he said: *"My father used to sing. Off-key. My mother pretended not to notice."* It is the most personal thing he has ever said to her. She plays it for him again the next night. He does not sing. He listens. It is enough.

### With Lyna

Mireille and Lyna share a warm, easy camaraderie. Lyna is practical and focused; Mireille is expressive and social. They are opposites who get along because neither tries to change the other.

Mireille teaches Lyna a rhythm to tap on her bow arm while waiting for shots — a steady beat that keeps her calm and focused. Lyna adopts it without comment. It becomes her pre-shot ritual. Mireille is delighted.

In combat, Mireille's buffs amplify Lyna's already devastating precision. A Lyna under Rallying Chorus is the party's single most dangerous damage source, and both of them know it.

**Key dynamic:** Lyna does not sing. She does not hum. She does not perform. But when Mireille plays the midsummer song, Lyna's rhythm-tapping matches the melody perfectly — she has been listening, always listening, and she knows the tempo by heart. Mireille notices. She does not comment. She plays the song again the next night.

### With Ione

Mireille and Ione share the most naturally musical relationship in the party. Ione hums dryad melodies — old, wordless, older than language. Mireille harmonizes with them. The combination is haunting and beautiful: human music and dryad music, two ancient traditions finding common ground.

They sit together at campfire, Ione humming, Mireille playing, and the sound draws the rest of the party in. Corin taps his crossbow stock. Silas nods along. Even Kaelen produces his approximately-C note. It is the closest thing the party has to a family moment.

In combat, Mireille's buffs and Ione's healing create the party's most powerful sustain window. Ione's Groveheart + Mireille's Rallying Chorus is the party's "push through anything" combination.

**Key dynamic:** Ione names Mireille "Gold-String" after the color of her viol's glow when she channels. Mireille names Ione "Root-Song" after the deep resonance of her humming. They do not use these names in front of others. It is their private language.

### With Brighid

Mireille and Brighid share a warm, energetic friendship. Both are loud, both are warm, both use food and music as their primary languages of care. They are the party's social engines — Mireille through song, Brighid through cooking. Together, they turn any campsite into a gathering.

Brighid cooks; Mireille plays. The combination is irresistible. The party learns that when Brighid is cooking and Mireille is playing, something good is happening. They gravitate toward the fire. They eat. They sing. They forget, briefly, that the world is ending.

In combat, Mireille's buffs amplify Brighid's already terrifying bear form. A buffed Brighid is the party's most aggressive threat generator, and Mireille makes sure she stays buffed.

**Key dynamic:** Brighid names one of her stews "Mireille's Gold" because the broth is the same color as Mireille's viol glow. Mireille names one of her melodies "Brighid's Roar" because the crescendo sounds like a bear charging. They are both very pleased with themselves.

### With Vesper

Mireille and Vesper share a complicated, fascinating relationship. Vesper is cool, analytical, and information-driven. Mireille is warm, expressive, and emotion-driven. They are opposites who are drawn to each other precisely because of the contrast.

Mireille is one of the few people who can make Vesper smile without intending to. She plays something unexpectedly beautiful, and Vesper's composure cracks for half a second. Mireille notices. She files it away. She plays it again later. Vesper pretends not to be affected.

In combat, Mireille's buffs and Vesper's debuffs create a powerful offensive combination. Mireille amplifies the party; Vesper weakens the enemy. Together, they shift the entire combat calculus.

**Key dynamic:** Vesper once asked Mireille, with genuine curiosity: *"Why do you play? The refugees you sang for — they were going to die anyway. What was the point?"* Mireille considered this. *"Because the singing mattered. Not because it saved them. Because it made the time they had better. That is the point."* Vesper was quiet for a long time. *"I do not understand. But I want to."* Mireille smiled. *"That is enough."*

## Loot and Equipment Identity

**Weapon: Rosethorne Viol**

Mireille's weapon is a curved, handheld string instrument — part musical tool, part magical focus, part weapon. The viol is made of warm-toned wood with a visible crack running along its lower bout, carefully glued and reinforced with thin gold wire. The tuning pegs are worn from years of use. The strings glow faintly when she channels — gold for buffs, dark crimson for debuffs. The crack is not repaired because Mireille refuses to erase the evidence of what she survived.

**Armor: Festival Singer's Ensemble**

Mireille's "armor" is her festival singer's aesthetic — a fitted bodice with embroidered floral patterns, a flowing skirt split for movement, a short capelet pinned at the shoulder with a brass lute-shaped pin. The outfit is enchanted for mobility and light protection, but it reads as performance wear, not combat gear. This is deliberate: Mireille fights as a performer, and her appearance reflects that.

**Equipment Sets:**

- **Wandering Minstrel Set (Early Game):** Travel-worn performance clothes, basic viol, simple capelet. Functional, faded, well-loved. Represents Mireille as the player finds her — talented but under-resourced, singing for refugees in barns.

The set bonus massively amplifies Festival of the Last Light, representing the way her grief and joy fuel her music. Visually, the viol's crack blazes gold during ultimate ability, and Mireille's entire ensemble takes on the warm glow of a midsummer bonfire.

## Party AI Behavior Notes

Mireille's RL agent is trained to maximize party effectiveness through buff timing, debuff placement, and tempo management. Her decision-making centers on reading the combat state and deploying the right skill at the right moment.

**Expertise 0.0 (Basic):**

- Casts Rallying Chorus on cooldown, prioritizing the highest-DPS party member as the channel center
- Uses Dirge of the Fallen when three or more enemies are clustered in a cone
- Places Bridge of Hearts on the tank and the lowest-HP party member
- Deploys Festival of the Last Light when more than half the party is below 50% health
- Does not account for buff stacking or decay timing

**Expertise 0.5 (Competent):**

- Times Rallying Chorus to coincide with party damage windows (boss vulnerability phases, ultimate ability alignment)
- Uses Dirge of the Fallen to disrupt telegraphed enemy attacks, not just clustered groups
- Places Bridge of Hearts strategically — linking tank with squishy for safety, or two damage dealers for burst windows
- Manages channel positioning — plants herself in protected locations, behind Kaelen's shield line
- Deploys Festival of the Last Light when the encounter is at its most critical, not just when the party is low
- Accounts for Conductor's Ear passive — prioritizes keeping party members alive to maximize buff duration

**Expertise 1.0 (Optimal):**

- Reads the full combat state and sequences buffs/debuffs for maximum multiplicative effect — Rallying Chorus timed with Ione's Groveheart, Dirge applied before Evelyn's AoE burst
- Manages buff stacking and decay with precision — refreshes Rallying Chorus one second before stacks expire, maintaining maximum uptime
- Places Bridge of Hearts dynamically — reassigns pairing based on real-time threat assessment, not static roles
- Uses channel positioning as a tactical tool — positions to hit maximum enemies with Dirge while staying protected, forces Mireille to be a positioning-aware combatant
- Deploys Festival of the Last Light with encounter-level awareness — saves it for boss enrage phases or overwhelming ambushes, never wastes it on trivial encounters
- Manages emotional energy cost — balances buff deployment with recovery windows, avoids over-committing and leaving the party without support during critical moments
- Reads silence threats — holds channel skills when silence-capable enemies are present, switches to instant-cast Bridge of Hearts as a fallback
